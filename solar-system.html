Solar System

The solar system consists of the Sun and the objects that orbit it, including planets, dwarf planets, moons, asteroids, and comets.

Sun

The Sun is the most massive object in our solar system and contains about 99.86% of all the mass in the entire solar system. It is a giant ball of hot plasma held together by its own gravity. The Sun is primarily composed of hydrogen and helium, which undergo nuclear fusion in its core, converting mass into the tremendous energy that powers the entire solar system.

Our Sun is approximately 4.6 billion years old and is roughly halfway through its life cycle. Every second, the Sun converts about 600 million tons of hydrogen into helium through nuclear fusion, releasing vast amounts of energy that travels to Earth as light, heat, and radiation.

Mercury

Mercury is the smallest planet in our solar system and the closest to the Sun. It has extreme temperature variations, with daytime temperatures reaching 427°C (800°F) and nighttime dropping to -173°C (-280°F). Mercury has no atmosphere to retain heat, and its surface is covered in craters similar to the Moon. It orbits the Sun every 88 Earth days, making it the fastest planet in our solar system.

Venus

Venus is the second planet from the Sun and is often called Earth's "sister planet" due to their similar sizes and masses. However, Venus is a harsh and inhospitable world. It has the hottest surface of any planet in our solar system, with temperatures reaching 465°C (869°F), hot enough to melt lead.

Venus has a thick atmosphere composed primarily of carbon dioxide with clouds of sulfuric acid, creating a runaway greenhouse effect. The atmospheric pressure on Venus is 92 times greater than on Earth, equivalent to being 3,000 meters deep in the ocean. Uniquely, Venus rotates backwards (retrograde rotation) and rotates so slowly that its day is longer than its year. It is the only planet that rotates from east to west when viewed from above the North Pole.

Earth

Earth is the third planet from the Sun and the only known planet in the universe to harbor life. It has a diverse biosphere with liquid water covering about 71% of its surface. Earth's atmosphere is composed mainly of nitrogen and oxygen, and it has a protective magnetic field that shields it from harmful solar radiation. Our planet takes 365.25 days to orbit the Sun and rotates on its axis every 24 hours, creating day and night cycles.

Moon

The Moon is Earth's only natural satellite and has a major influence on our planet's tides and axial stability. It is covered in a dusty, rocky surface and has phases that change as it orbits Earth every 27.3 days. The Moon has no atmosphere, so temperatures vary widely between day and night.

Mars

Mars is the fourth planet from the Sun and is often called the Red Planet because of its iron oxide-rich soil. Mars has the largest volcano in the solar system, Olympus Mons, and evidence suggests it once had flowing water on its surface.

Jupiter

Jupiter is the fifth planet from the Sun and the largest planet in our solar system. This gas giant is more than 11 times wider than Earth and has a mass greater than all the other planets combined. Jupiter's most distinctive feature is the Great Red Spot, a storm system larger than Earth that has been raging for at least 400 years. The planet has a complex system of bands and zones, which are clouds of different compositions moving at different speeds. Jupiter has at least 79 known moons, including the four large Galilean moons: Io, Europa, Ganymede, and Callisto.

Saturn

Saturn is the sixth planet from the Sun and is famous for its spectacular ring system. These rings are composed of countless particles of ice and rock, ranging in size from dust grains to house-sized chunks. The rings extend hundreds of thousands of kilometers from the planet but are surprisingly thin, with a thickness of only about 20 meters on average. Saturn is the second-largest planet in our solar system and is a gas giant composed primarily of hydrogen and helium. Despite being less dense than water, Saturn is a massive planet with at least 82 known moons, including Titan, which has a thick atmosphere and liquid methane lakes on its surface.

Uranus

Uranus is the seventh planet from the Sun and is an ice giant with a unique characteristic: it rotates on its side. Its axis of rotation is tilted at approximately 98 degrees, likely due to a massive collision early in its history. This unusual orientation means that Uranus experiences extreme seasonal changes, with each pole experiencing 42 years of continuous sunlight followed by 42 years of darkness. Uranus has a blue-green color due to methane in its atmosphere, which absorbs red light. The planet has at least 27 known moons and a faint ring system.

Neptune

Neptune is the eighth and farthest planet from the Sun in our solar system. It is also an ice giant, similar to Uranus, with a striking deep blue color caused by methane in its atmosphere. Despite being the most distant planet from the Sun, Neptune is not the coldest; it actually emits more heat than it receives from the Sun, suggesting it retains internal heat from its formation. Neptune has the strongest winds in the solar system, with speeds exceeding 2,100 kilometers per hour (1,300 miles per hour). It has at least 14 known moons, with Triton being the largest and most interesting, featuring geysers and a retrograde orbit.

Pluto

Pluto was long considered the ninth planet of our solar system until 2006, when the International Astronomical Union reclassified it as a "dwarf planet" due to its small size and the discovery of other similar objects in the Kuiper Belt. Pluto has a highly elliptical orbit and is significantly smaller than Earth's moon. Despite its small size, Pluto has an atmosphere, a system of moons (with Charon being the largest), and geological features including mountains and polar ice caps. The New Horizons spacecraft provided our first close-up images of Pluto in 2015, revealing a surprisingly complex and diverse world with evidence of past geological activity.
